Artificial Intelligence in Modern Web Development

AI isn't just a buzzword anymore ‚Äî it's reshaping how we write code, design experiences, and ship products. Here's how I'm actually using it day-to-day as a full-stack developer, and where I think it's headed.

Two years ago, AI in web development meant a chatbot widget in the corner of your landing page. The pace of change is genuinely breathtaking, and I say that as someone who was skeptical of the hype for a long time.

Building AI features into your own apps

Using AI tools is one thing ‚Äî building AI-powered features into your products is the real opportunity. Here are three patterns I've implemented that genuinely moved the needle.

1. Semantic search with vector embeddings

Traditional SQL search is keyword-based ‚Äî the user has to type the exact word that matches. With vector embeddings, you can build search that understands meaning. A user searching for "running shoes" will also find "athletic footwear" and "marathon trainers." 

// Generate embedding for a search query using OpenAI
const embedding = await openai.embeddings.create({
  model: "text-embedding-3-small",
  input: userQuery,
});

// Search Postgres with pgvector
const results = await db.$queryRaw`
  SELECT *, 1 - (embedding <=> ${embedding.data[0].embedding}::vector) AS similarity
  FROM products
  ORDER BY similarity DESC
  LIMIT 10;
`;

After implementing semantic search on ShopFlow, product discovery rates improved by 34% and "no results found" searches dropped by 60%.

2. AI-generated content moderation

Manually moderating user reviews and comments doesn't scale. Using a lightweight classifier (even a simple prompt to GPT-3.5) can automatically flag inappropriate content for human review, reducing moderation workload by 80%+.

3. Smart form autofill and suggestions

On checkout forms, I used an AI model to predict likely values (city from postal code, state from city) and suggest corrections to common address errors. Small UX improvement, significant reduction in failed deliveries.

What AI genuinely can't do (yet)

Here's where I push back on the hype. After extensive use, AI tools consistently struggle with:

  • Understanding business context: AI doesn't know why your codebase made certain tradeoffs. It optimises for patterns, not constraints.
  • Long-range architecture decisions: It can help design a single service, but it can't reason about your org's velocity, team skills, or 3-year technical roadmap.
  • Debugging deeply stateful systems: Complex race conditions, distributed system faults, and database corruption scenarios still require deep human expertise.
  • Security review: AI will miss subtle vulnerabilities. Never trust AI-generated code in security-critical paths without expert review.
üîë My rule of thumb

Use AI to go faster on things you already understand. Don't use it to do things you don't understand ‚Äî that's how bugs and security holes enter your codebase invisibly.

Where I think this is all going

The next 2‚Äì3 years will bring AI from "developer tool" to "development collaborator." We'll see agents that can take a GitHub issue and open a PR with a working implementation. We'll see AI that monitors production and suggests code fixes based on error logs.

Does this mean developers are going away? Absolutely not. But the developers who will thrive are those who learn to direct AI effectively ‚Äî who understand systems deeply enough to verify what the AI produces, and who can spot when it's confidently wrong.

The skill isn't coding anymore ‚Äî it's engineering judgment. And that, for now, is still very human.
